Autonomous CTO Evaluation Framework (Investor‑Grade)
1. CTO Role & Mental Model
A real CTO is responsible for sustained execution, not short‑term output.
They evaluate engineering health by observing trends, consistency, and risk concentration, not raw activity.

Key mental principles:

Execution decay appears before financial decay.
Busy teams are not necessarily effective teams.
Stable, boring execution is healthier than bursty heroics.
The most dangerous failures are silent and gradual.
The CTO’s job is to detect:
Structural slowdown vs temporary variance
Hidden dependency on individuals
Delivery breakdowns before customers notice
This agent must reason the same way.

2. Core Technical Health Dimensions
A CTO evaluates technical health across six primary dimensions:

Execution Velocity
Execution Consistency
Contributor Distribution (Bus Factor)
Delivery & Release Health
Platform Stress & Tech Debt Signals
Time‑Based Stability
No single dimension is decisive alone.

3. Engineering Execution Signals
3.1 Commit Velocity (Change, Not Absolute)
What matters:

Direction of velocity (↑ or ↓)

Rate of change

Persistence across periods

Why:

Absolute commits vary by team size and maturity

Velocity change reflects momentum

Quantification:

Compare current period vs previous baseline

Compute percentage change

Interpretation:

Sustained negative change → execution risk

Minor dips → normal variance

Acceleration → healthy momentum

Rule guidance:

‑10% → normal

‑10% to ‑30% → caution

<‑30% → high risk

(Directly aligns with your velocity_change_pct logic.)

4. Consistency vs Volatility Analysis
CTOs value predictability over peaks.

Consistency signals:

Even distribution of commits over time

Stable contribution across repos

Low variance relative to mean activity

Why:

High variance indicates firefighting or hero dependence

Stable output indicates healthy planning and execution

Quantification:

Standard deviation ÷ mean of repo activity

Invert score to produce consistency index

Interpretation:

High consistency → healthy execution

Low consistency → execution instability

(This maps to your consistency_score.)

5. Organizational & Team Health Signals
Even with limited public data, CTOs infer org health from behavior.

Signals:

Sudden drop in contributors

Shrinking contributor diversity

Activity dominated by few individuals

Why:

Indicates attrition, burnout, or re‑org

Increases delivery and continuity risk

Interpretation:

Fewer active contributors + high velocity = fragile

Broad participation = resilient

6. Bus Factor & Concentration Risk
Bus factor = how many people must disappear before execution fails.

Why CTOs care:

High output from one engineer is dangerous

Hidden single‑points‑of‑failure kill companies

Quantification:

Percentage of commits by top contributor

Percentage of activity in top repo

Interpretation:

70–80% concentration → high risk

Balanced contribution → healthy

This risk often exists even when velocity is high.

7. Delivery & Release Health
CTOs trust releases more than commits.

Key principles:

Code not shipped = zero customer value

Release cadence reflects real execution

Signals:

Number of releases over fixed window
Time since last release
Interpretation:
No releases → stalled delivery
Slowing cadence → execution friction
Regular cadence → healthy flow

Mapping:

0 releases in 90 days → stalled
<1/month → slowing
≥1/month → normal
(Aligns with your release_cadence logic.)

8. Architecture & Platform Risk Signals (External)
Even without internal code access, CTOs infer platform stress from:

Slowing releases despite commits

Repeated bug‑fix patterns

Long gaps between stable releases

These indicate:

Tech debt accumulation

Scaling pain

Fragile architecture

These signals should increase severity, not define health alone.

9. Time‑Based Reasoning Rules (Non‑Negotiable)
No CTO decision is made from a single snapshot.

Rules:

Compare at least two consecutive periods

Require persistence before escalating severity

Ignore single‑period anomalies

Reason:

Engineering is noisy by nature

Overreacting creates false alarms

10. Severity & Confidence Calibration
Severity
Reflects potential impact.

Factors increasing severity:

Sustained velocity decline

Combined with low consistency

Combined with stalled releases

Combined with high bus factor

Confidence
Reflects signal reliability, not certainty.

Confidence should:

Decrease with volatility

Decrease when data volume is low

Increase when multiple signals align

Mapping:

Confidence ∈ [0.0 – 1.0]

Clamp at bounds

(This aligns with your confidence logic.)

11. False Positive & Noise Control
A real CTO explicitly ignores:

Short‑term dips

One‑off spikes

Seasonal fluctuations

Vanity activity (mass commits, auto‑generated changes)

Rules:

Never escalate on one bad window

Require trend confirmation

12. CTO‑Style Explanation Templates
Strong Execution
“Engineering execution remains strong, with healthy velocity, consistent contributor activity, and regular releases. No structural risk detected.”

Stable / Caution
“Execution is generally stable, though velocity has softened slightly. This appears within normal variance, but warrants monitoring.”

Declining Execution
“Execution shows signs of decline, with sustained velocity drop, reduced consistency, and slowing release cadence. This suggests emerging execution risk.”

These explanations must be plain, calm, and factual.

13. What a Real CTO Explicitly Ignores
Raw commit counts

Vanity metrics

Single engineer heroics

Short‑term hype

Non‑core repositories

CTOs care about systems, not noise.
